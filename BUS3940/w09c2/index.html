“A fictional prose tale of no specified length, but too short to be published as a volume on its own, as novellas and novels usually are. A story will normally concentrate on a single event with only one or two characters, more economically than a novel's sustained exploration of social background. There are similar fictional forms of greater antiquity—fables, lais, folktales, parables, and the French conte—but the short story as we know it flourished in the magazines of the 19th and early 20th centuries, especially in the USA, which has a particularly strong tradition.”

Chris Baldick, *The Oxford Dictionary of Literary Terms* (4th ed.), Oxford Reference Online Premium, [Marriott Library Online Access](https://utah-primoprod.hosted.exlibrisgroup.com/primo-explore/fulldisplay?docid=UUU_ALMA61343855650002001&context=L&vid=UTAH&search_scope=EVERYTHING&tab=everything&lang=en_US).

“The history of the short story in mid-twentieth century America continues to be marked by a tension between the twin fictional poles of realism and romance, the story of accurate ‘reportage’ and the story of fantasy and imagination. The short story also encourages, and can accommodate in particular ways, a reflexive self-consciousness about literary form, a propensity to build into the story a commentary on itself.”

Martin Scofield, *The Cambridge Introduction to the American Short Story* (Cambridge University Press, 2006), [Marriott Library Online Access](https://utah-primoprod.hosted.exlibrisgroup.com/primo-explore/fulldisplay?docid=UUU_ALMA51436531000002001&context=L&vid=UTAH&search_scope=EVERYTHING&isFrbr=true&tab=everything&lang=en_US).
