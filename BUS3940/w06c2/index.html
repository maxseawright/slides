“an imitation, synchretic”4 is as fair a distillation as any of the issues surrounding usage of the word synchretic.

4. Capistrano de Abreu, Primeira Visitação do Santo Ofiício, cited in Mário de Andrade, Macunaíma, 2a ed. (Paris: ALLCA XX, 1996), 545.

The chapter “Macumba” serves, in part, to set up Macunaíma’s conversion to another synchretic religion, Caraimonhaga, despite its apparents disappearance long before the time of Macunaíma’s publishing in 1928, in a series of synchretic, mixed, cannibalistic steps toward a new Brazilian aesthetic.

“aesthetic: a set of principles underlying and guiding the work of a particular artist or artistic movement: the Cubist aesthetic.” New Oxford American Dictionary.

Like many modernists, Mário de Andrade can be described as hostile to tradition, even Afro-Brazilian or indigenous traditions. He uses caricatures of their practices and beliefs for aesthetic purposes.

My hope is that, independent of synchretic origins of particular religious traditions, if you choose to comment or write about religions on quizzes, homework, or papers, you can do so academically, not with aesthetic irreverence like Mário de Andrade’s.

...Not that you must use William James’ Varieties of Religious Experience (1902) as a guide, but consider its distinction between a religious tradition’s relevance through its origin and history versus its proposition of value as “importance, meaning, or significance” to its followers.5
