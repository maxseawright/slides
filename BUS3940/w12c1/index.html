A caricature of Delfim Netto, “Czar of the Economy” (Ziraldo 1980, Globo Archive). A childish Delfim Netto plays with a Cruzeiro coin, Brazil’s then currency. Ziraldo’s drawing references the upbeat song “Lá vem o Brasil descendo a ladeira,” released by Moraes Moreira in 1979. The original lyrics are altered slightly in the cartoon, meaning “there goes Brazil, down the hill,” a reference to the Cruzeiro’s declining worth, rather than the original lyric’s idealization of a woman descending stairs metonymically representing all of Brazil, “here comes Brazil down the hill.” The Cruzeiro’s annual inflation rate was above 110% in 1980.

The takeaway

* Military dictatorship was not able to sustain Brazil’s ‘Economic Miracle’ after the second oil-shock of 1979. * Pharaonic projects did not have the lasting, positive impact on development that some had hoped. * Inflation targeting was only made formal policy in 1990s, [first in New Zealand](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/research-and-publications/speeches/2018/speech2018-04-12) and eventually [becoming ‘gospel’](https://www.nytimes.com/2014/12/21/upshot/of-kiwis-and-currencies-how-a-2-inflation-target-became-global-economic-gospel.html). * Brazil only regained currency stability through its [transition to the Real in 1994](https://www.npr.org/sections/money/2015/12/02/458222801/episode-216-how-four-drinking-buddies-saved-brazil).
