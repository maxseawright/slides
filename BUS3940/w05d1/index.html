What are traditional Amazonian cultivars?

palms (açaí)

fruit trees (avocado, guava)

annual crops (cassava, plantains, corn, sweet potatoes)

Global Forest Atlas, “Traditional Land Use and Shifting Cultivation”

What is shifting cultivation?

“Because of the nutrient limitations, cultivation of these annual crops is often limited to just a few cycles. After this, the farm plot will be allowed to succeed into a perennial crop or ‘forest garden’ type of landscape, with shade tolerant perennial crops such as coffee, cacao, and other fruit trees, or long lived useful species such as rubber and Brazil nut can be harvested for many years to come. In many cases, farmers will return to these forest gardens after several decades to clear and cultivate again.”

(emphasis mine) Global Forest Atlas, “Traditional Land Use and Shifting Cultivation”

“Agroforestry integrates agriculture with timber species and high diversity forest”

Global Forest Atlas, “Agroforestry in the Amazon”

Notes on translation

Some of the experimental language—“prose rhapsody”—of the original text is translated in way that one might describe as edgy. The first phrase uttered by Macunaíma is, “what a fucking life!” in our translation. The original is “que preguiça!” [“what sloth!” as in the sense, “I’m so tired, fam.”]

Auxiliary notes on translation

You may find the linked conversation with Natasha Wimmer, Roberto Bolaño’s English translator, illuminating. How to translate rhetorical elements, cadence, and culturally relevant information (e.g. the “semi-false cognate,” in Wimmer’s words, at 11:54 in the interview) represents an enormous creative task.
